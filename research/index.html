WhatsApp & Women's Livelihoods: Digital Practices and Economic Participation
Digital Economy Gender & Women's Empowerment Livelihoods Financial Inclusion India Kenya Nigeria Pakistan

Examines how women across multiple countries use WhatsApp in their daily economic lives — analyzing barriers and drivers of adoption, and how the platform shapes pathways into formal and informal economic participation.

Outputs
  • Report
    WhatsApp and Women's Livelihoods: How Everyday Digital Practices Shape Pathways to Economic Participation Caribou Digital Publishing, 2026
    This report examines how women across India, Kenya, Nigeria, and Pakistan use WhatsApp to support income-generating activities. Drawing on nationally representative surveys of 7,000 adults and qualitative interviews, it estimates that approximately 80–98 million working-age women across these four countries rely on WhatsApp for livelihoods. The study analyzes adoption patterns, usage depth, and barriers to effective platform engagement, finding a pronounced income gradient in effective use and identifying how gender gaps in device ownership and business access shape women's economic participation through digital platforms.

COVID-19, Food Security & Gender: Phone Survey Evidence from LMICs
Food Security Gender & Women's Empowerment Public Health COVID-19 Survey Methodology Bangladesh Kenya Nigeria

A longitudinal phone survey study tracking household food security, employment, mental health, and gender outcomes across three rounds in Bangladesh, Kenya, and Nigeria during and after COVID-19. Examines who gets reached by phone surveys, how food insecurity and mask-wearing behavior evolved, and how women's vulnerabilities changed over time.

Outputs
  • Journal Article
    Do Referrals Improve the Representation of Women in Mobile Phone Surveys? Journal of Development Economics, 2023
    Random digit dial surveys with mobile phones risk under-representation of women. To address this, we compare the characteristics of women recruited directly with those of women recruited through referrals from male household members. The referral process improves representation of vulnerable groups, such as young women, the asset poor, and those living in areas with low connectivity. Among mobile phone users, we show a referral (rather than a direct dial) protocol includes more nationally representative proportions of women with these attributes. While seeking intra-household referrals may improve representation, we show that it does so at a higher cost.

  • Journal Article
    Food Insecurity and COVID-19 Risk: Insights from Mobile Phone Surveys in Bangladesh, Kenya, and Nigeria Applied Economic Perspectives and Policy, 2022
    The COVID‐19 pandemic prompted social distancing, workplace closures, and restrictions on mobility and trade that had cascading effects on economic activity, food prices, and employment in low‐ and middle‐income countries. Using longitudinal data from Bangladesh, Kenya, and Nigeria covering a period from October 2020 to April 2021, the paper assesses whether knowledge of a person infected with COVID‐19 is associated with food insecurity, job loss and business closures, and coping strategies to smooth consumption. The likelihood of households to experience food insecurity at the extensive and intensive margins increased among those who knew an infected person in Bangladesh and Kenya.

  • Journal Article
    Unmasking the Truth: Experimental Evidence of Facemask Compliance in Bangladesh, Kenya, and Nigeria during the COVID-19 Pandemic PLOS Global Public Health, 2023
    High levels of compliance with public health measures are critical to ensure a successful response to the COVID-19 pandemic and other public health emergencies. However, most data on compliance are self-reported and the tendency to overreport due to social desirability could yield biased estimates of actual compliance. A list experiment is a widely used method to estimate social desirability bias in self-reported estimates of sensitive behaviours. We estimate rates of compliance with facemask mandates in Kenya, Nigeria, and Bangladesh using data from phone surveys conducted in March-April 2021. We find large gaps between stated and elicited rates of facemask wearing: an almost 40 percentage point gap in Kenya, 30 percentage points in Nigeria, and 20 percentage points in Bangladesh. Data collected from self-reported surveys may not be reliable to monitor ongoing compliance with public health measures.

Causal Identification of Remote Data Collection Bias in Food Security Measurement
Food Security Humanitarian Settings Survey Methodology Myanmar Ecuador Central African Republic Burkina Faso

A randomized experiment embedded in multi-country household surveys to causally identify how remote data collection modes affect food security measurement. By randomly assigning households to phone or face-to-face interview modes, the study estimates the causal effect of mode on reported food consumption, coping strategies, and expenditure outcomes — contributing to a growing literature on measurement equivalence in hard-to-reach populations.

Intra-Household Gender Differences in Food Security Reporting: A Cross-over Experiment in Iraq
Food Security Gender & Women's Empowerment Humanitarian Settings Survey Methodology Iraq

A paired cross-over experiment conducted across IDP and refugee camps in Iraq, in which each sampled household was interviewed twice — once with a male respondent and once with a female respondent from the same household. The design enables direct within-household comparison of food security reporting by sex, measuring minimum dietary diversity (MDD), the reduced Coping Strategies Index (rCSI), and a newly developed gender-sensitive rCSI module identifying who within the household applies each food coping strategy. The study contributes to a broader literature on respondent-sex effects on standard food security indicators and their implications for gender-sensitive data collection.
